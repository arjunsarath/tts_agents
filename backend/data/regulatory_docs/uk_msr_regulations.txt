UK Money Service Business (MSB) Regulations

Source: Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), Payment Services Regulations 2017 (PSR 2017), Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017)

REGULATORY AUTHORITY
The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) is the primary regulator for payment service providers and money service businesses in the United Kingdom. HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) maintains the MSB register for smaller operators.

LICENSING AND REGISTRATION
Under the Payment Services Regulations 2017:
- Payment institutions must be authorized by the FCA
- Small payment institutions (less than EUR 3 million monthly average) may register instead of full authorization
- Electronic money institutions require separate FCA authorization
- All money service businesses must register with HMRC for anti-money laundering supervision

Authorization Requirements:
- Minimum initial capital of EUR 125,000 for payment institutions
- Fit and proper person assessment for directors and beneficial owners
- Business plan demonstrating adequate governance and risk management
- Professional indemnity insurance or comparable guarantee
- Adequate safeguarding measures for customer funds

CUSTOMER DUE DILIGENCE (KYC)
Under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017:

Standard Due Diligence:
- Full name
- Date of birth
- Residential address
- Verification using reliable, independent sources (e.g., passport, driving license, utility bills)

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) Required for:
- Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)
- High-risk third countries (as per HM Treasury list)
- Complex or unusual transactions
- Transactions above GBP 15,000 (approximately)
- Customers from jurisdictions with weak AML frameworks

Simplified Due Diligence Allowed for:
- Low-risk products and services
- Certain regulated financial institutions
- Government bodies and public authorities

TRANSACTION MONITORING AND REPORTING
- Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) must be filed with the National Crime Agency (NCA)
- No specific monetary threshold for SARs — based on suspicion
- Cross-border wire transfers must include originator and beneficiary information per FATF Rec 16
- Transfers to/from sanctioned countries must be blocked

SANCTIONS COMPLIANCE
- UK maintains its own sanctions regime (post-Brexit) under the Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018
- Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) administers financial sanctions
- All transactions must be screened against the UK Sanctions List
- Breach of sanctions is a criminal offense with penalties including imprisonment

KEY CORRIDORS FOR UK REMITTANCES
The UK is a major source of outbound remittances:
- UK → India (largest corridor by volume)
- UK → Pakistan
- UK → Nigeria
- UK → Bangladesh
- UK → Ghana
- UK → Morocco
- UK → Philippines
- UK → Poland
- UK → Jamaica

SAFEGUARDING REQUIREMENTS
Payment institutions must safeguard customer funds through one of:
- Segregation in a separate account at an authorized credit institution
- Investment in secure, liquid, low-risk assets
- Insurance policy or comparable guarantee

RECORD KEEPING
- All KYC documentation: retain for 5 years after the business relationship ends
- Transaction records: retain for 5 years from the date of the transaction
- Training records: retain for 5 years
- Risk assessments: keep up to date and available for inspection

RECENT REGULATORY DEVELOPMENTS (2023-2024)
- FCA Consumer Duty implementation affecting payment firms
- Enhanced scrutiny of authorized push payment (APP) fraud protections
- New rules for crypto-asset financial promotions
- Strengthened requirements for sanctions screening
- Focus on operational resilience for payment firms
- Updated guidance on treatment of PEPs

PENALTIES FOR NON-COMPLIANCE
- FCA can impose unlimited financial penalties
- Criminal prosecution for serious AML failures
- Directors can face personal liability and prohibition orders
- Potential loss of authorization/registration
- Public censure and enforcement notices
